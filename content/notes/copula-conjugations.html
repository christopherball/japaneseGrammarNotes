This note summarizes the core conjugation patterns for the Japanese copula across plain, polite, honorific, and written explanatory forms.

The polite negative column includes both the more formal では forms and the conversational じゃ variants that often appear in everyday speech.

Written Explanatory Forms

In writing, the following forms may appear when the author is explaining or presenting something in a more formal expository register.

Form Plain Polite
Non-past である であります
Past であった でありました

Plain Form Examples

The table below gives representative plain-style examples for the core forms above, including both the standard では negatives and the conversational じゃ variants.

Function Japanese English
Non-past (affirmative) 彼は学生だ。 He is a student.
Non-past (negative) 彼は学生ではない。 He is not a student.
Non-past (negative, casual) 彼は学生じゃない。 He is not a student.
Past (affirmative) 彼は学生だった。 He was a student.
Past (negative) 彼は学生ではなかった。 He was not a student.
Past (negative, casual) 彼は学生じゃなかった。 He was not a student.
Volitional / conjectural 彼は学生だろう。 He is probably a student.
Continuative (連用形) 彼は学生で、彼女は先生だ。 He is a student, and she is a teacher.
Conditional (なら) 彼が学生なら、この本を知っているはずだ。 If he's a student, he should know this book.
Conditional (ならば) 彼が学生ならば、図書館にいるかもしれない。 If he's a student, he might be in the library.

Notes

  • で is the continuative (連用形) form of the copula and can connect clauses, including weak causal links depending on context.
  • なら and ならば behave as conditional forms in plain style, while であれば serves the more formal parallel.
